A typical evaporative condenser has various design components, these are explained below.

Condensing Coil Arrangement

These coils, usually made from corrosion-resistant copper or cupro-nickel when used in ammonia systems, are arranged in a serpentine or finned-tube configuration to maximize surface area for heat transfer. The serpentine design allows for efficient refrigerant flow, while finned tubes enhance air-side heat exchange.

Water Sump and Distribution System

The water sump acts as a reservoir for the recirculating water. A suction strainer prevents debris from entering the water pump, which ensures a steady flow of water to the spray header. The spray header, positioned above the coils, houses numerous low-pressure nozzles that distribute water evenly across the entire coil surface, promoting complete wetting of the tubes for optimal heat transfer.

Fans or Blowers

Axial propeller fans are commonly used due to their ability to move large volumes of air efficiently. Alternatively, squirrel cage blowers can be employed in some designs. These fans draw air upwards through the wet coils, facilitating the evaporative cooling process.

Drift Eliminators

These are strategically placed mesh pads that capture water droplets dragged in the air stream exiting the condenser. This minimizes drift, and the unwanted loss of water droplets carried away by the air, reducing water consumption and potential environmental concerns.

Make-up and Bleed-off Water Supply

A make-up water line replenishes water lost due to evaporation. Regular bleed-off, where a portion of the sump water is replaced with fresh water, helps to control mineral buildup, and maintain water quality for optimal performance.
